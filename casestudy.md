# Pocket, book & go!

## The Beginning
"A book you read on a train is not the same book you read at a desk."
This project started with a simple request from a client: a book small enough to slip into a pocket. Book design ready to read on the tram, on the train, in the in-between moments of the way.
What sounded like a format constraint turned into a great design thinking experience. A pocket isn't just a size, it's a context. It implies one hand, short attention spans, bad lighting, and a reader who can be interrupted at any stop.
I worked from cover to the text: format, typography, layout, navigation inside the text, and the final print-ready file.

---
## How to make it work?
A pocket book is a small object with a lot of constraints stacked on top of each other:
The page is narrow, so line length is limited
The paper is thin, so show-through is a real risk
The binding has to open flat enough to read one-handed,
the reader is moving, distracted, and rarely has both hands free.
Most pocket editions ignore at least one of these. They shrink a normal book down and hope it works. It usually doesn't, the type gets too dense, the margins disappear, and the reader gives up after two stops.

---
## How I made it work

##### First, I built the format:
I tested the trim size tested against actual jacket and coat pockets, after that
chose a paper weight to balance bulk and opacity.
To finish the physical design process I tested binding for one-handed reading.

##### Then, I built the page:
I created a simple one serif type system inside, with a modern sans serif type cover.
Line length tuned to the narrow column with a generous outer margin so a thumb has somewhere to rest, also used for in text notes.

##### Then, the details:
Chapter openings that work as visual breakpoints

---

## The major points that I build my work on:
* Comfort — the page should not fight the reader
* Orientation — the reader should always know where they are
* Object — the book should feel like something worth carrying

## What I Learned with such small format
Designing a small book is not the same as designing a small version of a book.
Key lessons:

* Constraint is a brief, not a limit — the pocket size dictated almost every good decision in the project
* Test the object, not just the page — a layout that works in InDesign can fall apart in a coat pocket
* Failures are cheaper early — catching the underline problem in the concept phase saved it from going to print

Working with a client on a print project sharpened my decisions. There's no undo on a print run, and that pressure made every choice more deliberate.

---

## Summary
This was a focused exercise in designing for a specific reading context rather than a generic one. The pocket format forced clarity at every level: format, type, navigation, and finish. The result is a small book that takes its smallness seriously.
Beyond the deliverable, the project deepened my understanding of how typography, format, and context shape a reader's experience and how a single bad decision (like the wrong note style) can ripple through the whole page.
