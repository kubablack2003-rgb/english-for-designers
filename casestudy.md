# Pocket, book & go!

## The Beginning

> *"A book you read on a train is not the same book you read at a desk."*

This project started with a simple request from a client: a book small enough to slip into a pocket. A book designed to be read on the tram, on the train, in the in-between moments of the way.

What sounded like a format constraint turned into a real design problem. A pocket isn't just a size, it's a context. It implies one hand, short attention spans, bad lighting, and a reader who can be interrupted at any stop.

I worked from cover to text: format, typography, layout, navigation inside the text, and the final print-ready file.

---

## How to make it work?

A pocket book is a small object with a lot of constraints stacked on top of each other:

- The page is narrow, so line length is limited
- The paper is thin, so show-through is a real risk
- The binding has to open flat enough to read one-handed
- The reader is moving, distracted, and rarely has both hands free

Most pocket editions ignore at least one of these. They shrink a normal book down and hope it works. It usually doesn't — the type gets too dense, the margins disappear, and the reader gives up after two stops.

---

## How I made it work

##### First, I built the format:
I tested the trim size against actual jacket and coat pockets, then chose a paper weight to balance bulk and opacity. To finish the physical design process I tested the binding for one-handed reading.

##### Then, I built the page:
I created a simple one-serif type system inside, with a modern sans-serif on the cover. Line length was tuned to the narrow column, with a generous outer margin so a thumb has somewhere to rest.

For in-text notes, I first tested an underline style — but it competed with the body text and pulled the eye away from the sentence instead of supporting it. I replaced it with a quieter solution that stays out of the reader's way.

##### Then, the details:
Chapter openings that work as visual breakpoints. Running heads that double as orientation markers, so the reader always knows where they are after looking up from the page.

---

## The major points I built my work on:

* **Comfort** — the page should not fight the reader
* **Orientation** — the reader should always know where they are
* **Object** — the book should feel like something worth carrying

## The result

A reader can carry the book without thinking about it, read one-handed on a moving train, lose their place and find it again in seconds, and finish a chapter between two stops.

> *"The book stops being a thing you sit down with. It becomes a thing you live alongside."*

## What I learned with such a small format

Designing a small book is not the same as designing a small version of a book.

Key lessons:

* **Constraint is a brief, not a limit** — the pocket size dictated almost every good decision in the project
* **Every typographic signal costs something** — the underline notes failed because they spent attention the body text needed
* **Test the object, not just the page** — a layout that works in InDesign can fall apart in a coat pocket
* **Failures are cheaper early** — catching the underline problem in the concept phase saved it from going to print

Working with a client on a print project sharpened my decisions. There's no undo on a print run, and that pressure made every choice more deliberate.

---

## Summary

This was a focused exercise in designing for a specific reading context rather than a generic one. The pocket format forced clarity at every level: format, type, navigation, and finish. The result is a small book that takes its smallness seriously.

Beyond the deliverable, the project deepened my understanding of how typography, format, and context shape a reader's experience — and how a single bad decision (like the wrong note style) can ripple through the whole page.
