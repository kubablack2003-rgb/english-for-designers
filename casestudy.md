# Pocket Book – Case Study

## The Beginning

This project started with a simple request from a client: a book small enough to slip into a pocket. Something to read on the tram, on the train, in the in-between moments of a commute.

What sounded like a format constraint turned into a design problem. A pocket isn't just a size — it's a context. It implies one hand, short attention spans, bad lighting, and a reader who can be interrupted at any stop.

I worked end-to-end on the concept: format, typography, layout, navigation inside the text, and the final print-ready file.

> "A book you read on a train is not the same book you read at a desk."

---

## Project Brief

The brief had three core elements:

- A format that fits in a standard pocket
- A reading experience built for short, fragmented sessions
- A visual identity that holds together across the whole book

I handled the project end-to-end — concept, typesetting, layout system, cover, and prepress. The final outcome is a complete, print-ready concept.

---

## The Core Problem Or Opportunity

> "The goal was not to redesign the book. It was to redesign the reading moment."

A pocket book is a small object with a lot of constraints stacked on top of each other:

- The page is narrow, so line length is limited
- The paper is thin, so show-through is a real risk
- The binding has to open flat enough to read one-handed
- The reader is moving, distracted, and rarely has both hands free

Most pocket editions ignore at least one of these. They shrink a normal book down and hope it works. It usually doesn't — the type gets too dense, the margins disappear, and the reader gives up after two stops.

I focused on three main opportunities:

**Readability in motion**
Typography sized and spaced for a moving vehicle, not a quiet study.

**Orientation**
Clear chapter markers and page anchors so a reader can find their place after being interrupted.

**Object quality**
A book that feels good in the hand — not a paperback shrunk in a photocopier.

---

## Understanding Reality

Before designing, I needed to understand how people actually read on the move.

I looked at:

- How readers hold a book on public transport (almost always one-handed)
- How often they look up (every few seconds)
- Which existing pocket editions readers actually finish
- How typography behaves at small sizes on cheap paper

> "Readers don't want smaller books. They want books that survive being put down."

That insight reframed the whole project. The design wasn't about miniaturization — it was about resilience. Every spread had to let the reader leave and come back without losing the thread.

---

## Design Process

> "I moved from object to page to detail."

**First, I built the format:**

- Trim size tested against actual jacket and coat pockets
- Paper weight chosen to balance bulk and opacity
- Binding tested for one-handed reading

**Then, I built the page:**

- Type system: one serif for the body, one sans for navigation
- Line length tuned to the narrow column
- Generous outer margin so a thumb has somewhere to rest

**Then, the details:**

- Chapter openings that work as visual breakpoints
- Running heads that double as orientation markers
- A cover that reads from across a train car

These priorities guided every decision:

- **Comfort** — the page should not fight the reader
- **Orientation** — the reader should always know where they are
- **Object** — the book should feel like something worth carrying

---

## What Worked, What Didn't

Some things landed:

- The trim size genuinely fits a jacket pocket without bulging
- The type holds up at the small size, even on textured paper
- Chapter markers make it easy to resume after an interruption

Other things didn't:

| Area | Status | Insight |
|---|---|---|
| Underlined notes | Didn't work | The underline style I tested for marginal notes competed with the body text. It pulled the eye away from the sentence instead of supporting it. Replaced with a quieter solution. |
| Footnote density | Partial | On note-heavy spreads, the page still feels crowded. Worth revisiting in a second edition. |
| Cover finish | Open | The current concept assumes uncoated stock. A more durable finish might serve the use case better. |

The underline experiment was the most useful failure. It taught me that on a small page, every typographic signal has to earn its space — and a note system that competes with the main text isn't a note system, it's a distraction.

---

## The Impact

The final outcome is a print-ready concept that treats the pocket format as a design problem in its own right — not a shrunken edition of something larger.

A reader can:

- Carry the book without thinking about it
- Read one-handed on a moving train
- Lose their place and find it again in seconds
- Finish a chapter between two stops

> "The book stops being a thing you sit down with. It becomes a thing you live alongside."

---

## What I Learned

Designing a small book is not the same as designing a small version of a book.

Key lessons:

- **Constraint is a brief, not a limit** — the pocket size dictated almost every good decision in the project
- **Every typographic signal costs something** — the underlined notes failed because they spent attention the body text needed
- **Test the object, not just the page** — a layout that works in InDesign can fall apart in a coat pocket
- **Failures are cheaper early** — catching the underline problem in the concept phase saved it from going to print

Working with a client on a print project sharpened my decisions. There's no undo on a print run, and that pressure made every choice more deliberate.

---

## Summary

This was a focused exercise in designing for a specific reading context rather than a generic one. The pocket format forced clarity at every level — format, type, navigation, and finish. The result is a small book that takes its smallness seriously.

Beyond the deliverable, the project deepened my understanding of how typography, format, and context shape a reader's experience — and how a single bad decision (like the wrong note style) can ripple through the whole page.
